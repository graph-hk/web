# **What's Next**
## Making Sense of the Autocratization Process in Hong Kong

**Date**: September 27, 2024

**Time**: 10:30 pm Hong Kong Time; 9:30 am Central Time; 10:30 am Eastern Time; 7:30 am Pacific Time

**Speaker**: Eliza Lee, University of Hong Kong

**Discussant**: Ray Yep, University of Bristol

**[Registration](https://forms.gle/VaVzUJ34ogwTu5Pp6){:target="\_blank"} Required**

{% include countdown.html %}

Unlike many recent cases of autocratization, which tends to be a gradual process, Hong Kong experienced an abrupt autocratic turn following a significant social insurgency in 2019. In this talk, I will focus on Beijing’s brutal ending of Hong Kong’s free election in 2020, and analyze the decision through the unique characteristics of the Hong Kong political regime. The governing coalition in Hong Kong is not endogenously formed. The Chinese party-state (or its agent) is the primary allocator of political spoils, and on which incumbents are politically dependent for their continued inclusion in the coalition. The Chinese party-state inherited a city with strong opposition parties and strong civil society upon the sovereignty handover. Provisions of the Basic Law were “hard constraints” on the party-state, creating a rare situation of prolonged confrontation between a strong authoritarian state and a strong civil society. The anti-ELAB movement culminated in a perceived crisis of regime breakdown for Beijing, with political opposition coalescing to attempt an electoral breakthrough in the 2020 Legislative Council election and the support of the pro-business members toward the executive impaired by the much unpopular amendment to the Fugitive Offenders Ordinance. Accordingly, the party-state’s subsequent revamp of the electoral systems in 2020 serves not only to demobilize the opposition parties and civil society but also to significantly dilute the influence of the businesspeople,  thus increasing the safety margin against elite defection.

#### **Speaker**

<img align="right" src="{{site.baseurl | prepend: site.url}}images/picSpeakerLee.jpg" alt="speaker" width="150"/> [Eliza W.Y. Lee](https://ppaweb.hku.hk/faculties/name/eliza){:target="_blank"} is currently an honorary professor in the Department of Politics and Public Administration at The University of Hong Kong, where she was also a professor from 2006 to 2023. She has published widely on various topics of Hong Kong politics and public administration, including political regimes, civil society, social movement, state-society relations, civic engagement, collaborative governance, public management, social policy development, and gender. Her articles have appeared in Journal of Contemporary China, Governance, Policy and Politics, International Public Management Journal, Journal of Social Policy, Public Administration Review, Journal of Civil Society, Voluntas, and Asian Survey. She is the lead author of Public Policymaking in Hong Kong: Civic Engagement and State-Society Relations in a Semi-Democracy (2013) and the editor of Gender and Change in Hong Kong: Globalization, Postcolonialism, and Chinese Patriarchy (2003). She currently serves on the editorial board of Voluntas and International Review of Administrative Sciences, and previously served on Public Administration Review. She was head of the department (2013-2016) and director of its Centre for Civil Society and Governance (2010 – 2018). Her past public positions include: member of the Humanities and Social Sciences Panel, Research Grant Council (2021-2023); assessor of the Research Assessment Exercise 2020 Social Science Panel, Research Grant Council, HKSAR; elected member of the Higher Education Sector of the Election Committee, HKSAR (2007-2017). She is currently writing a book manuscript on societal mobilization for democracy in Hong Kong.
